Preimage:

Despite the notation \(f^{-1}(G)\), \(f\) is not assumed to be injective or surjective, so \(f\) may not be invertible. However, if \(f\) is invertible, then \(f^{-1}\) is in fact the inverse of \(f\).

Continuity on X A function \(f:X\to Y\) is continuous on \(X\) if

\[ \forall x\in X\ \forall \varepsilon >0\ \exists \delta >0\quad d_X(x,y)<\delta \implies d_Y\big (f(x),f(y)\big )<\varepsilon \]

In contrast, for uniform continuity on \(X\),

\[ \forall \varepsilon >0\ \exists \delta >0\ \forall x\in X\quad d_X(x,y)<\delta \implies d_Y\big (f(x),f(y)\big )<\varepsilon \]

so \(\delta \) does not depend on \(x\).

Uniform continuity may be considered a step towards differentiability.

The theorem fails if \((X,d_X)\) is not compact. See the counterexample

\[ f:(0,1)\to \R ,\quad x\mapsto \frac 1x \]

This is continuous but not uniformly continuous. The idea behind this counterexample is to have a problem at an endpoint, but not the middle. Closer to \(x=0\), the \(\delta \) required needs to be smaller.

\[ \delta (x)\to 0\quad \text {as}\quad x\to 0 \]

A bounded counterexample is

\[ f(x)=\sin \frac 1x \]

This is the composition of two continuous functions, so is continuous, but it is not uniformly continuous for the same reason, which can be shown from the definition.
