Intuitively, a contraction moves points closer together, by at least some set proportion. The contraction mapping theorem then shows a contraction mapping has a unique fixed point.

Picard-Lindelöf is close to the lecturer’s heart. Contains a some technical assumptions.

Lipschitz continuous is on the way to differentiability. So it is “differentiable almost everywhere” see further courses.

You get a local solution.

For the proof, you need to show the space is complete.

  • Example 1. From the 2021 exam, slightly modified.

    Let \(\mathcal {K}\) be the set of all non-empty compact subsets of \((\R ,\abs {\cdot })\). Define

    \[ d(X,Y)=\max \left (\sup _{x\in X}\inf _{y\in Y}\abs {x,y},\sup _{y\in Y}\inf _{x\in X}{\abs {x-y}}\right ),\ X,Y\in \mathcal {K} \]

    This defines a metric on \(\mathcal {K}\) and \((\mathcal {K},d)\) is complete (Q3). (See the diagram for a visualisation.)

    Define \(T:\R \to \R \) by \(T(x)=\frac {x}3\). \(T\) is a contraction on \((\R ,\abs {\cdot })\) with \(c=\frac 13\), and \(T\) is a contraction on \((\mathcal {K},d)\) with

    \[ T(X)=\{T(x):x\in X\}\quad \text {for}\quad X\in \mathcal {K} \]

    \(T:\mathcal {K}\to \mathcal {K}\) since \(T(X)\) is compact for \(X\in \mathcal {K}\). (Result from week 5, since \(T\) is a contraction and contractions are continuous.)

    \(T:\mathcal {K}\to \mathcal {K}\) is also a contraction with

    \[ d\big (T(X),T(Y)\big )\le \frac 13d(X,Y),\quad X,Y\in \mathcal {K} \]

    to show this, wrestle with the definition of \(d(X,Y)\).

    Now define \(\mathcal {T}:\mathcal {K}\to \mathcal {K}\) by

    \[ \mathcal {T}(X)=T(X)\cup \left (\frac 23+T(X)\right ) \]

    This is also a contraction on \((\mathcal {K},d)\) with \(c=\frac 13\) (proof omitted). Hence it has a unique fixed point \(K\in \mathcal {K}\) such that

    \[ \mathcal {T}(K)=K\qquad K=\frac 13K\cup \left (\frac 23+\frac 13 K\right ) \]

    \(K\) is the “middle third Cantor set”, which is a “fraction” with dimension \(\frac {\log 2}{\log 3}\in (0,1)\). This is similar to a Sierpinski triangle. The metric is the Hausdorff metric.
