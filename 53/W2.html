  • Example 1. Presentations give a useful way to represent a group. They consist of generators and relations.

    \[ \langle x\mid x^6=1\rangle \]

    “The largest group with the given generators satisfying the given relations”

    An example of the group above is

    \(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

    \begin{align*} C_1&=\{1\}\\ C_2&=\{1,-1\}\subseteq \C ^\times \\ C_3&=\{1,e^{2\pi i/3},e^{4\pi i/3}\}\subseteq \C ^\times \\ C_6&=\{e^{2k\pi i/6}\mid 0\le k\le 5\} \end{align*} Clearly all of these satisfy the relation given above, but only \(C_6\) actually has that presentation. Some other groups with this presentation are

    \(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

    \begin{gather*} \text {Rot}(P_6),\quad \text {the rotations of a hexagon}\\ \left (\Z /6\Z ,+,0\right )=\{0,1,2,3,4,5\},\quad \text {generators:}(1,5)\\\left (\Z /7\Z \right )^\times =\{1,2,3,4,5,6\},\quad \text {generators:}(3,5) \end{gather*} Consider the generators of each group. Clearly each have two; \(x,x^5\).

  • Example 2. Now consider

    \[ \left (\Z /15\Z \right )^\times =\{1,2,4,7,8,11,13,14\} \]

    This is of order 8. Not all elements are of this order. Try to find these

    \[ 2,\ 2^2=4,\ 2^3=8,\ 2^4=1 \]

    \(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

    \begin{align*} \abs {1}&=1\\ \abs {2}&=4 \end{align*} From lectures, for \(g\in G\),

    \[ \abs {g}=n\implies \abs {g^k}=\frac {n}{\hcf (k,n)} \]

    So

    \(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

    \begin{align*} \abs {4}&=2\\ \abs {8}&=4 \end{align*}

    \[ 7,\ 7^2=4,\ 7^3=13,\ 7^4=1 \]

    So

    \(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

    \begin{align*} \abs {7}&=4\\ \abs {13}&=4 \end{align*} and

    \[ 11^2=1,\ 14^2=1 \]

    So

    \(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

    \begin{align*} \abs {11}&=2\\ \abs {14}&=2 \end{align*} Hence

    Elements Order
    1 1
    4,11,14 2
    2,8,7,13 4

    What is the presentation of this group?

    \[ \{2^a11^b\mid 0\le a\le 3,\ 0\le b\le 1\} \]

    Primitive group is generated by a single generator, unlike this group.

    \[ \langle x,y\mid x^4=y^2=1,\ yx=xy \rangle \]

    The final equality in this presentation si commutativity, showing this group is abelian.

  • Example 3. Consider the symmetries of a square. Take the two operations of a flip (over a particular axis) and a rotation (clockwise)

    \[ \Sym (P_4)=\{r^af^b\mid 0\le a\le 3,\ 0\le b\le 1\} \]

    So

    \[ D_8=\langle x,y\mid x^4=y^2=1,\ yx=x^3y \rangle \]

    By induction or otherwise, it can be shown

    \[ yx^i=x^{3i}y \]

  • Example 4. Consider the presentations

    \(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

    \begin{align*} G_1&=\langle x,y\mid x^2=y^2=(xy)^2=1\rangle \\ G_2&=\langle x,y\mid x^7=y^5=1,\ yx=x^3y\rangle \\ G_3&=\langle x,y\mid x^3=y^3=(xy)^3=1\rangle \end{align*}

    • \(\protect \boldsymbol {G_\arabic {enumi}}\).

      \(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

      \begin{align*} xyxy&=1\\ x^2yxy^2&=x1y\\ yx&=xy \end{align*} Hence this group is abelian, and its elements are

      \[ \{1,x,y,xy\} \]

      This is the Klein 4-group \(C_2\times C_2\). An example of this is

      \[ (\Z /8\Z )^\times =\{1,3,5,7\} \]

    • \(\protect \boldsymbol {G_\arabic {enumi}}\). As before,

      \[ yx^i=x^{3i}y \]

      and similarly (by induction),

      \[ y^jx=x^{3^j}y^j \]

      So

      \(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

      \begin{align*} y^5x&=x\\ x^{3^5}y^5&=x\\ x^{242}&=1=x^7\\ x&=1 \end{align*} so

      \[ G_2=\langle y\mid y^5=1\rangle \]

    • \(\protect \boldsymbol {G_\arabic {enumi}}\).

      Imagine a triangle on an infinite plane, with corners labelled anti-clockwise \(1,2,3\), as above. Let \(a\) be a rotation of \(2\pi /3\) (anti-clockwise) about vertex 1, and \(b\) be the same about 2. Then \(ab\) is a rotation of \(4\pi /3\) (anti-clockwise) about 3, and so \(x^3=y^3=(xy)^3=1\).

      Now consider \(\gamma =ab^2\). As in the diagram, this translates the triangle away from its starting location, without changing its rotation. By repeating \(\gamma \), the triangle may get as far away from its starting location as desired. Therefore, this group must be infinite.
