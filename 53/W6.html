Theorem (Sylow). For group \(G\), prime \(p\), let

\[ \abs {G}=p^km\quad k\ge 1\quad p\nmid m \]

Define

\[ \text {Syl}_p(G)=\{H:H\le G,\ \abs {H}=p^k\}\quad \text {and}\quad n_p=\abs {\text {Syl}_p(G)} \]

Then

  • (1) \(n_p\ge 1\), so \(\exists H\le G\ \abs {H}=p^k\).

  • (2)

    \[ H\le G\ \abs {H}=p^t\ \text {and}\ Q\in \text {Syl}_p(G)\implies \exists g\in G\quad gHg^{-1}\subseteq Q \]

  • (3)

    • (a) \(n_p\equiv 1\mod p\)

    • (b) \(n_p\mid m\)

This may be considered a partial converse to Legrange’s theorem. It gives information about the subgroups of a group, specifically, by looking at the prime factors of the order of the group.

If \(H\le G\) and \(H\) is unique of order \(d=\abs {H}\), then \(gHg^{-1}\) which is also a subgroup of order \(d\) must equal \(H\), so \(H\normal G\).

  • Example 1 (Groups of order 28). Let \(G\) be a group of order \(28=2^2\times 6\). Consider the prime factors in turn

\(p=7\) Sylow (1) givese that \(n_7\ge 1\). Sylow (3) gives

\[ \begin {array}{l} n_7\equiv 1\mod 7\\ n_7\mid 4 \end {array}\implies n_7=1 \]

So there exists unique Sylow 7-subgroup

\[ \exists ! K\le G\quad \abs {K}=7,\ K\cong C_7 \]

\(p=2\) Sylow (1) gives \(n_2\ge 1\).

\[ Q\le G,\quad \abs {G}=4 \]

so

\[ Q\cong C_4\quad \text {or}\quad Q\cong C_2\times C_2 \]

Since \(4\) and \(7\) are coprime,

\[ K\cap Q=\{1\}\implies K\normal G,\ Q\le G\quad \text {and}\quad \abs {G}=\abs {K}\cdot {Q} \]

So this is a semi-direct product

\[ G\cong C_7\rtimes _\theta Q \]

Firstly, taking \(Q\cong C_4\) gives

\[ \theta :C_4\to \Aut (C_7)\cong C_6 \]

Under \((\Z /7\Z )^\times \),

\[ 3^2=2,\ 3^3=6,\ 3^4=4,\ 3^5=5,\ 3^6=1 \]

so we have generator 3. Note we only really need to check that \(3^2\) and \(3^3\) aren’t the identity. So

\(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

\begin{gather*} \phi _r:C_7\to C_7,\quad x\mapsto x^r\\ \langle \phi _3\rangle =\Aut (C_7)\\ \end{gather*} For \(C_4=\langle y:y^4=1\rangle \),

\(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

\begin{gather*} \theta :C_4\to \Aut (C_7),\quad y\mapsto \theta _y,\quad \theta _y^4=\phi _1=\id \end{gather*} So we have the possibilities

  • (i) \(\theta _y=\phi _1\)

  • (ii) \(\theta _y=\phi _1\), so

    \[ \theta _y=\phi _3^3=\phi _{27}=\phi _{-1} \]

    Then

    \[ G_1=\langle x,y:x^7=y^4=1,\ yx=xy=C^7\times C^4\cong C_{28} \]

    which is abelian, or

    \[ G_2=\langle x,y:x^7=y^4=1,\ yx=x^{-1}y \]

    which is non-abelian.

Now consider \(Q=C_2\times C_2\). As before, \(\langle \phi _3\rangle =\Aut (C_7)\), but we need

\[ \theta :C_2\times C_2\to \Aut (C_7)=\langle \phi _3\rangle \cong C_6 \]

We need to show where both generators of \(Q=C_2\times C_2\) go.

\[ \theta _y^2=\phi _1=\theta _z^2 \]

We have the possibilities

\[ (\theta _y,\theta _z)=\begin {cases} (\phi _1,\phi _1)\\ (\phi _{-1},\phi _1)\\ (\phi _1,\phi _{-1})\\ (\phi _{-1},\phi _{-1}) \end {cases} \]

So this gives the groups

\(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

\begin{align*} G_3&=\langle x,y,z: z^7=y^2=z^2,\ yz=zy,\ yx=xy,\ zx=xz\rangle =C_7\times C_2\times C_2\\ G_4&=\langle x,y,z: z^7=y^2=z^2,\ yz=zy,\ yx=x^{-1}y,\ zx=xz\rangle \\ G_5&=\langle x,y,z: z^7=y^2=z^2,\ yz=zy,\ yx=xy,\ zx=x^{-1}z\rangle \\ G_6&=\langle x,y,z: z^7=y^2=z^2,\ yz=zy,\ yx=x^{-1}y,\ zx=x^{-1}z\rangle \end{align*} \(G_3\) is the only one of these that is abelian. Clearly \(G_4\) and \(G_5\) are the same. By swapping \(y\) and \(yz\), so too is \(G_6\).

  • Example 2. Consider groups \(G\) of order \(42=2\times 3\times 7\) with an element of order 6.

    Sylow for \(p=7\) gives

    \[ n_7\equiv 1\mod 7,\quad n_7\mod 6 \]

    so \(n_7=1\) and so

    \[ \exists K\normal G\quad K\cong C_7 \]

    Let \(y\in G\), \(\abs {y}=6\). Then \(Q=\langle y\rangle \cong C_6\). Since \(7,6\) are coprime,

    \[ K\cap Q=\{1\},\ K\normal G,\ Q\le G,\ 42=\abs {G}=\abs {K}\abs {q} \]

    So we have the semi-direct product

    \[ G\cong K\rtimes _\theta Q,\quad \theta :C_6\to \Aut (C_7)\cong C_6\cong \langle \phi _3\rangle ,\quad y\mapsto \theta _y \]

    where \(\theta _y^6=\phi _1\). So \(\theta _y\) has possible orders \(1,2,3,6\), so this gives the cases

    \[ \theta _y=\begin {cases} \phi _1\\ \phi _{-1}=\phi _3^{3}\\ \phi _{3}^2=\phi _2\quad \text {or}\quad \phi _3^4=\phi _4\\ \phi _3\quad \text {or}\quad \phi _3^{-1}=\phi _5 \end {cases} \]

    The two possibilities for each order will clearly give isomorphic groups. So our groups are

    \(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

    \begin{align*} G_1&=\langle x,y:x^7=y^6=1,\ yx=xy\rangle \cong C_7\times C_6\cong C_{42}\\ G_2&=\langle x,y:x^7=y^6=1,\ yx=x^{-1}y\rangle \\ G_3&=\langle x,y:x^7=y^6=1,\ yx=x^2y\rangle \\ G_4&=\langle x,y:x^7=y^6=1,\ yx=x^3y\rangle \end{align*}

  • Example 3. Each of the groups \(G_i\) above has a unique \(K_i\normal G_i\) with \(\abs {K}=7\). So define

    \[ c_i:G_i\to \Aut (K_i)\quad g\mapsto c_G\quad c_g:K_i\to K_i\quad K_i\mapsto gK_ig^{-1} \]

    How large is \(\abs {\im (c_i)}\)? For \(G_3\), \(\{\phi _2,\phi _4,\phi _1\}\), and in fact, \(\abs {\im (c_i)}=i\).
