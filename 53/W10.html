We consider two examples for polynomials. For these we need the important results

  • Theorem 0.1 (Eisenstein’s Criterion). Let \(q(x)=a_nx^n+\cdots +a_1x+a_0\),

Being irreducible and having proper factorisation are not the same thing. Consider \(2x-2=2(x-1)\).

  • Theorem 0.2 (Gauss’). Let \(f(x)\in \Z [x]\) be primitive. Then \(f(x)\) is irreducible over \(\Z \) if and only if \(f(x)\) is irreducible over \(\Q \).

Question 1. Is \(x^4+7x^3+24x^2+37x+22\) irreducible over \(\Q \)?   

We may not directly apply Eisenstein, but we may do a translation \(f(x+c),\ c\in \Z \) to find the answer. This may be done with a computer using the nd the following code: 



      f(x)=x^4+7*x^3+24*x^2+37*x+22
      for i in range (-1,3):
          print(expand(f(x+i)))

We find that

\[ f(x-1)=x^4+3x^3+9x^2+6x+3 \]

so we may use Eisenstein with \(p=3\). So there is no proper factorisation of \(f(x-1)\) over \(\Z \), so no proper factorisation of \(f(x)\) over \(\Z \). \(f\) is monic and Gauss so \(f(x)\) is irreducible over \(\Q \).

Question 2. I missed the question but still. Probably find the factorisation of \(x^{12}-1\).   

We have

\(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

\begin{gather*} x^n-1=\prod _{d\mid n}\Phi _d(x)\\ \Phi _d(x)=\prod _{\mu \text { primitive for }d}(x-\mu ) \end{gather*} so

\[ x^{12}-1=\Phi _1\Phi _2\Phi _3\Phi _4\Phi _6\Phi _12 \]

We may calculate

\(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

\begin{align*} \Phi _1&=x-1\\ \Phi _2&=x+1\\ \Phi _3&=\frac {x^3-1}{x-1}=x^2+x+1\\ \end{align*}

\(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

\begin{align*} x^4-1=(x^2-1)(x^2+1)&\implies \Phi _4=x^2+1\\ x^6-1=(x^3-1)(x^3+1)&\implies \Phi _6=\frac {x^3+1}{x+1}=x^2-x+1\\ x^{12}-1=(x^6-1)(x^6+2)&\implies \Phi _{12}=\frac {x^6+1}{x^2+1}=x^4-x^2+1 \end{align*} These are all irreducible over \(\Q \). \(\Phi _1,\Phi _2\) are linear and \(\Phi _3,\Phi _4,\Phi _6\) are quadratic with no real roots. For \(\Phi _{12}\), \(x^4-x^2\ge 0\) over \(\Z \), so \(\Phi _{12}\ge 1\). Therefore there are no roots in \(\Z \) so no linear factors over \(\Z \).

If it may be factorised into two quadratics \(f(x),g(x)\), then \(\Phi _{12}(x)=\Phi _{12}(-x)=f(-x)g(-x)\) so \(f(-x),g(-x)\) are also factors. But we only have two quadratic factors, so either \(f(x)=f(-x)\) or \(f(-x)=g(x)\). For the second case, for some \(a,b\in \Z \),

\(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

\begin{align*} x^4-x^2+1&=f(x)f(-x)=(x^2+ax+b)(x^2-ax+b) \end{align*} form the \(x^2\) coefficient, \(-1=2b-a^2\) and from the constant coefficient, \(1=b^2\), so \(b=\pm \). Then \(a^2=3,-1\). But this would give \(a\not \in \Z \), which is a contradiction. Otherwise, for some \(b,c\in \Z \),

\(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

\begin{align*} x^4-x^2+1&=(x^2+b)(x^2+c)\\ &=x^4+x^2(b+c)+bc \end{align*} Then \(b+c=-1\) and \(bc=1\), but this is not possible for \(b,c\in \Z \). Hence \(\Phi _{12}\) is irreducible in \(\Z \) and hence irreducible in \(\Q \).
