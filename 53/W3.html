</h1> <a id="W3-autopage-1"></a> <p> <span class="paragraph" id="autosec-2">Subgroups</span> <a id="W3-autopage-2"></a> Let \(G\) be a group If \(H\subseteq G\) and \(H\) is also a group under the same operation, then \(H\) is a subgroup of \(G\), we write \(H\le G\). </p> <p> <span class="paragraph" id="autosec-3">Homomorphism</span> <a id="W3-autopage-3"></a> \(f:G\to H\) is a homomorphism if and only if </p> <p> \[ \forall a,b\in G\quad f(ab)=f(a)f(b) \] </p> <p> An Isomorphism is a bijective homomorphism, for which we write \(G\cong H\). </p> <p> Consider the cyclic group </p> <p> \[ C_8=\langle x:x^8=1\rangle \] </p> <p> This has subgroups </p> <span class="hidden"> \(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)</span> <!-- ⟨1⟩ = {1} = C1 ⟨x2 ⟩ = {1, x2 , x4 , x6 } = ⟨x6 ⟩ ∼ = C4 ⟨x4 ⟩ = {1, x4 } =∼ C2 C8 = ⟨x⟩ = ⟨x3 ⟩ = ⟨x5 ⟩ = ⟨x7 ⟩ --> <p> \begin{align*} \langle 1\rangle &=\{1\}=C_1\\ \langle x^2\rangle &=\{1,x^2,x^4,x^6\}=\langle x^6\rangle \cong C_4\\ \langle x^4\rangle &=\{1,x^4\}\cong C_2\\ C_8&=\langle x\rangle =\langle x^3\rangle =\langle x^5\rangle =\langle x^7\rangle \end{align*} In this case we could easily write down every subgroup. </p> <p> Now consider the dihedral group </p> <p> \[ D_8 =\langle x,y:x^4=y^2=1,\ yx=x^3y\rangle \] </p> <p> This has cyclic subgroups </p> <span class="hidden"> \(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)</span> <!-- ⟨1⟩ = {1} ⟨y⟩ = {1, y} ∼ = C2 ⟨x⟩ = {1, Both elements are of order 2, unlike \(x\), which is interesting. </p> <span class="hidden"> \(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)</span> <!-- xyx2 y = x7 y 2 = x3 x3 xy = y --> <p> \begin{align*} xyx^2y&=x^7y^2=x^3\\ x^3xy&=y \end{align*} Listing every subgroup is quite a tedious task. More likely, one may be asked to classify them, or explain why one is isomorphic to another. </p> <p> Take \(S_n\), the symmetric group of degree \(n\), the set of all bijections from the integers \(1,2,\dots ,n\) to themselves. An element may be written in two row notation </p> <p> \[ \sigma =\begin {pmatrix}1&2&3&4&5&6\\4&3&4&1&2&6\end {pmatrix} \] </p> <p> or in disjoint cycle notation </p> <p> \[ \sigma =(1\ 4)(2\ 3\ 5)(6) \] </p> <p> (Note that this notation is more standard than that which was used in previous courses, with commas.) This is useful since disjoint cycles commute. Therefore, the order is the lowest common multiple of the length of the cycles. </p> <p> \[ \abs {\sigma }=\text {lcm} (2,3)\varsigma \] </p> <p> \(\sigma \) has cycle structure \([1,2,3]\) </p> <p> <span class="paragraph" id="autosec-4">Cayley’s Theorem</span> <a id="W3-autopage-4"></a> If \(G\) is a group, \(\abs {G}=n\), then \(G\cong H\le S_n\). </p> <div class="amsthmproof"> <a id="W3-autopage-5"></a> <ul style="list-style-type:none"> <li> <p> <span class="amsthmproofname">Sketch of proof.</span> Take \(\lambda :G\to S_G\), where \(S_G\) is the set of bijections \(G\to G\).
