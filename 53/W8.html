Rings Today we move onto the second part of the course; Rings. We will look at why one may be interested in rings. Particularly, we look at fields, with which you may be more familiar. Fields are examples of rings.

A ring may be written

\[ (R,+,0,\cdot ,1) \]

This has an embedded abelian group

\[ (R,+,0) \]

and a multiplicative monoid (associative + identity)

\[ (R,\cdot , 1) \]

and the standard distributivity law

\(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

\begin{align*} \forall x,y,z\in \R \quad \begin{split}x(y+z)&=xy+xz\\(x+y)z&=xz+yz \end {split} \end{align*} where \(x\cdot y\) may be written \(xy\). Both equations must be given because commutativity is not assumed. \(M_n(\Z )\) \(n\ge 2\) is a non-commutative example of a ring.

Units The units of a ring \(R\) are

\[ R^\times =\{r\in R:\exists s\in R\ rs=1=sr\} \]

thus units are those elements with multiplicative inverses.

  • Example 1.

    • − \(\Z \) is a commutative ring with units \(\Z ^\times =\{+1,-1\}\).

    • − \(R=\F [x]\) is a ring with units \(\F [x]^\times =\F ^\times \). This is because

      \[ pq=1\implies \deg (pq)=\deg (1)\implies \deg (p)+\deg (q)0 \]

Fields A commutative ring \(R\) is a field if and only if \(R^\times =R-\{0\}\). So every non-zero element is a unit.

The integers \(\Z \) are clearly a ring, but instead consider the integers modulo \(n\).

  • − \(\Z /n\Z \) are the group of integers modulo \(n\) under \(+\)

  • − \((\Z /n\Z )^\times \) are the multiplicative group of units modulo \(n\) under \(\times \)

  • − \(\Z /(n)\) is the ring of integers modulo \(n\) under \(+\) and \(\times \), where

    \[ (n)=n\Z =\{an:a\in \Z \} \]

    This is an additive subgroup of \(\Z \), and

    \[ \forall a\in \Z \ \forall i\in (n)\quad ai\in (n) \]

    This may be considered a stronger form of closure.

  • Example 2. Consider

    \[ \Z /(6)=\{0+I,1+I,2+I,3+I,4+I,5+I\}\quad \text {where}\quad I=(6) \]

    We can multiply elements such as

    \[ (2+I)(3+I)=0+I \]

    Notice we are multiplying two non-zero elements to get zero, which shows that \(\Z /(6)\) is not a field, or more specifically, not an integral domain.

This gives a stronger condition for a ring to be a field.

  • Theorem. If \(R\) is a finite commutative integral domain, then \(R\) is a field.

  • Example 3. Consider

    \[ \F _3[x]=\{p(x):\exists n\ge 0\ a_0,\dots ,a_n\in \F _3\quad p(x)=a_0+a_1x+\cdots +a_nx^n\} \]

    Then take the subgroup

    \[ I=(x^2+1)=\{a(x)(x^2+1):a(x)\in \F _3[x]\} \]

    Now do the same thing as we did with the integers earlier. Consider “Polynomials over \(F_3\) modulo \(x^2+1\)”. Here any polynomial \(p(x)\in \F _3[x]\) may be expressed in this set by using polynomial division to get

    \[ p(x)=q(x)(x^2+1)+r(x) \]

    The remainder \(r(x)\) is then the important part. But \(\deg (r(x))\ge 1\), so the possible elements are just

    \[ \{0,1,2,x,x+1,x+2,2x,2x+1,2x+2\} \]

    Later on we will be able to see which polynomials like \((x^2+1)\) we can do this with. What we therefore get is a field with 9 elements.

    Let us justify that this is an integrable domain by doing some calculations.

    \(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

    \begin{align*} x^2&=(x^2+1)+2=2\\ x^3&=2x\\ x^4&=2x^2=2x^2+2+1=1\\ x^5&=x \end{align*} so \(x\) has order \(4\).

    \(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

    \begin{align*} (x+1)^2&=x^2+2x+1=2x\\ (x+1)^3&=2x(x+1)=2x^2+2x=2x^2+2+2x+1=2x+1\\ (x+1)^4&=(x+1)(2x+1)=2x^2+1=2x^2+2+1+1=2\\ \vdots &\\ (x+1)^8&=1 \end{align*} \((x+1)\) generates all of \(S-\{0\}\) where

    \[ S=\text {``Ring of polynomials over $\F _3$ modulo $x^2+1$''} \]

    So every elements \(s\in S-\{0\}\) may be written \(s=(x+1)^i\), so

    \[ s^{-1}=(x+1)^{8-i} \]

    Thus \(S^\times =S-\{0\}\) so \(S\) is a field.

Instead, consider infinite fields.

  • Example 4. Let \(\alpha _1,\dots ,a_k\in \C \) and \(\Q [\alpha _1,\dots ,\alpha _k]\) be the smallest ring containing \(\Q \) and \(\alpha _1,\dots ,\alpha _k\). This is definitely a ring because in the worst case scenario, it is just \(\C \).

    Consider

    \[ \Q [\sqrt {2}]=\{a+b\sqrt {2}:a,b\in \Q \} \]

    Is this actually true? Is the right hand side actually the smallest such ring? If \(p(x)in \Q [x]\) then \(p(\sqrt {2})\in \Q [\sqrt {2}]\). Therefore

    \(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

    \begin{align*} a_0+a_1\sqrt {2}+a_2\sqrt {2}^2+\cdots +a_n\sqrt {2}^n=\begin{split} &a_0+2a_2+4a_4+\cdots \\ &\sqrt {2}(a_1+2a_3+\cdots ) \end {split}\in \{a+b\sqrt {2}:a,b\in \Q \} \end{align*} This is a special case of a theorem

  • Theorem. If \(R\) is a commutative integral domain containing a field \(\F \) as a subring, then

    • 1. \(R\) is a vector space over \(\F \).

    • 2. If \(\dim _\F (R)\) is finite, then \(R\) is a field.

  • Sketch of proof.

    • 1. Think of the ring as an abelian group, with linearly independent spanning basis. In the example, this would be \(\{1,\sqrt {2}\}\).□

  • Example 5. Consider \(R=\Q [x]\). This is a vector space over \(\Q \) with infinite dimension, since it has potential basis

    \[ \{1,x,x^2,x^3,\dots \} \]

    This is not a proof.

    • Proof. Suppose \(\dim _\Q (\Q [x])=n\). Then there exists a basis \(\{p_1(x),\dots ,p_n(x)\}\), so any polynomial may be written as a linear combination of these. But then any polynomial with a larger degree than any of the basis polynomials cannot be written with the basis.

      \(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

      \begin{align*} t&=\max _{1\le i\le n}\deg (p_i)+1\\ x^t&=\sum _{i=1}^n \alpha _ip_i(x)\quad \alpha _i\in \Q \\ \deg (x^t)&=t>\deg \sum x_ip_i \end{align*} This is a contradiction, hence the ring has infinite dimension. □
