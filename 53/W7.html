  • Definition (Group Actions). Let \(G\) be a group and \(X\) be a set. A (left) group action of \(G\) on \(X\) is a map

    \[ \cdot :G\times X\to X\quad (g,x)\mapsto g\cdot x \]

    satisfying

    \(\seteqnumber{0}{}{0}\)

    \begin{alignat} {3} \text {Identity:}&&\qquad \forall x\in X&&\quad 1\cdot x&=x\\ \text {Compatibility:}&&\qquad \forall x\in X\ \forall g,h\in G&&\quad g\cdot (h\cdot x)&=(gh)\cdot x \end{alignat}

Group axioms are almost more fundamental than groups. Cayley’s theorem may be phrased in terms of group axioms. Notice that \(gh\) and \(g\cdot h\) are two different types of “multiplication”, and should not be confused. Right group axioms also exist, but are not needed for this course.

  • Definition (Orbits). For an action \(\cdot :G\times X\to X\) and \(x\in X\), the orbit of this action

    \[ G\cdot x=\{g\cdot x:g\in G\}\subseteq X \]

    This is the set of elements that may be reached using an action.

It is trivial to prove that \(x\sim y\iff y\in G\cdot x\) is an equivalence relation on \(X\). Therefore,

\[ X=\bigcap _{i=1}^m G\cdot x_i \]

and

  • Definition (Stabilizer Subgroup). Let \(x\in X\). The Stabilizer Subgroup is

    \[ G_x=\{g\in G:g\\cdot x=x\} \]

    This is in fact a subgroup.

  • Theorem (Orbit-Stabiliser Theorem).

    \[ \forall x\in X\quad \abs {G\cdot x}\abs {G_x}=\abs {G} \]

  • Definition (Simple Groups). A group \(G\) is simple if and only if \(G\) has no proper non-trivial normal subgroups.

  • Example 2. If \(p\) is prime, then \(C_p\) is simple, since it has no non-trivial subgroups.

It is fairly easy to show a group is not simple, one only has to show the existence of a normal subgroup.
