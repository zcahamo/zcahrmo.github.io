Question 6. Consider \(\Z [\sqrt {d}]\) where

  • (i) \(d\in \Z \)

  • (ii) \(d\) is square-free

  • (iii) \(d\equiv 1 \mod 4\)

  • (iv) \(d\ne 1\)

Show

  • (a) 2 is irreducible in \(\Z [\sqrt {d}]\)

  • (b) \(2\) is not prime in \(\Z [\sqrt {d}]\)

Hence \(\Z [\sqrt {d}]\) is not a UFD   

  • (i) Use proof by contradiction. Assume

    \[ 2=\alpha \cdot \beta \]

    for \(\alpha ,\beta \in \Z [\sqrt {d}]\) not a unit. Then

    \[ N(2)=N(\alpha )N(\beta ),\quad N(\alpha ),N(\beta ) \ne \pm 1 \]

    but

    \[ N(2)=(2+0\sqrt {d})\cdot (2-0\sqrt {d})=4 \]

    and \(N(\alpha ),N(\beta )\in \Z \) hence \(N(\alpha )=N(\beta )=+2\) or \(N(\alpha )=N(\beta )=-2\)

    Write

    \[ \alpha =x+y\sqrt {d},\quad x,y\in \Z \]

    Then

    \[ N(\alpha )=x^2-dy^2=\pm 2 \]

    Let us show this is impossible more strongly modulo 4.

    \(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

    \begin{align*} x^2-dy^2&\equiv x^2-y^2\mod 4\\ \end{align*} But \(x^2\) and \(y^2\) can only be \(0\) or \(1\) modulo 4, so this is 0,1 or \(-1\) modulo 4. Hence we have a contradiction

  • (ii) Let us construct \(\alpha ,\beta \in \Z [\sqrt {d}]\) such that

    \(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

    \begin{gather*} \alpha \cdot \beta \in 2\cdot \Z [\sqrt {d}]=\{2m+2n\sqrt {d}:m,n\in \Z \}\\ \alpha ,\beta \not \in 2\Z [\sqrt {d}] \end{gather*} Take \(\alpha =\beta =1+\sqrt {d}\). Then \(\alpha \not \in 2\Z [\sqrt {d}]\). Thus

    \[ \alpha \cdot \beta =(1+\sqrt {d})^2=1+d+2\sqrt {d} \]

    But \(1+d\) is even since \(1+d\equiv 2\mod 4\).

Question 5. Let \(p\) be a prime and consider \(\Z [\eta _p]\) with \(\zeta _p=e^\frac {2\pi i}{p}\). Choose \(f(x)\in \Z [x]\). Then show that if \(f(x)=0\) has a solution in \(\Z [\zeta _p]\) then

\[ \forall \text {prime }q\equiv 1\!\!\!\mod p\ \ \forall m\in \Z _{\ge 0}\ \exists x\in \Z /(q^\Z )\quad f(x)\equiv 0\mod q^m \]

  

Show the sub-result that if \(q\equiv 1\mod 4\) and \(m\in \Z _{\ge 0}\) then there exists a map

\[ \Z [\eta _p]\to \Z /(q^m\Z )\quad \alpha \mapsto \frac {\alpha }{\alpha } \]

such that

\(\seteqnumber{0}{0.}{0}\)

\begin{align*} \bar {1}&=1\in \Z /(q^m\Z )\\ \forall \alpha ,\beta \\in \Z [\sqrt {d}]\quad \begin{split} \overline {\alpha +\beta }&=\bar {\alpha }+\bar {\beta }\\ \overline {\alpha \cdot \beta }&=\bar {\alpha }\cdot \bar {\beta } \end {split} \end{align*} I have no idea what this \(\frac \alpha \alpha \) thing is, I think it is a function or something. This is a homomorphism of rings.

Then a corollary of this is that if \(k\in \Z \) then \(k\equiv k\mod q^n\). More generally,

\[ \forall h(x)\in \Z [x],\ \forall \alpha \in \Z [\zeta _p]\quad \overline {h(\alpha )}=h(\bar {\alpha }) \]

In particular,

\[ \bar 2=\overline {1+1}=\bar 1+\bar 1=1+1=2\in \Z /(q^m\Z ) \]

Write

\[ h(x)=a_0+a_1x+\cdots +a_d X^d \]

then

\[ \overline {h(\alpha )}=\overline {\alpha _0+\cdots }=d(\alpha ) \text { or something i'm not sure} \]

Thus to prove the question, let \(\alpha \in \Z [\zeta _p]\) be a solution of \(f(x)=0\). Then

\[ f(\bar {\alpha })=\overline {f(\alpha )}=\bar {0}=0 \]

so \(\bar {\alpha }\) is a solution of \(f(x)=0\) in \(\Z /(q^m\Z )\)

How do you construct this homomorphism? From analysis we know this map, if it exists, is uniquely determined by \(\epsilon :=\overline {\zeta _p}\). If \(\alpha \in \Z [\zeta _p]\) is written (uniquely) as

\[ \alpha =a_0+a_1\zeta _p+\cdots +1_{p-2}\zeta _p^{p-2} \]

then

\[ \bar {\alpha }=a_0+a_1\epsilon +\cdots +a_{p-3}\epsilon ^{p-2} \]

we also have that

\[ \zeta _p^p=1\quad \text {so}\quad \bar {1}=\overline {\zeta _p^p}=\epsilon ^p=1\in \Z /(q^m\Z ) \]

furthermore,

\[ \Phi _p(\zeta _p)=0\quad \text {so}\quad \Phi _p(\epsilon )=0 \]

We also have the lemma that If \(a=1\mod p\) and \(m\in \Z _{\ge 0}\) then

\[ \exists \epsilon \in (\Z /q^m\Z )^\times \epsilon ^p=1 \]

\[ \exists \epsilon \Z /(q^m\Z )\quad \Phi _p(\epsilon )=0 \]

  • Proof. (Sketch) Let \(g\in (\Z /qZ)^\times \) be a primitive root. Then the order of \(g\) is \(q-1\) in \((\Z /q\Z )^\times \). Since \(q\equiv 1\mod p\), \(\frac {q-1}{p}\in \Z \), we also know the order of \(T(g)\) in \(\Z /(q^m\Z )\) is \(q-1\). Thus \(\epsilon T(g)^\frac {1-q}p\) has order \(p\). □
