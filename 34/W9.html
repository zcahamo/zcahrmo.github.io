This week concerns the rings of integers, which acts as an introduction to algebraic number theory.

A ring of integers is

\[ R=\begin {cases} \Z [\sqrt {d}]&\quad d=2,3\mod 4\\ \Z \left [\frac {1+\sqrt {d}}2\right ]&\quad 1\mod 4 \end {cases} \]

for square-free (otherwise the square would just come out).

Note that

\[ N\left (\frac {1+\sqrt {d}}2\right )=\frac {1+\sqrt {d}}{2}\frac {1-\sqrt {d}}{2}=\frac {1-d}4 \]

so to remember the conditions, remember that this is only in \(\Z \) if \(d\equiv 1\mod 4\). On the other hand, for \(\Z [\sqrt {d}]\), \(x^2-d=0\) must have a solution.

\(R=\Z [\sqrt {-2}]\) is Euclidean with respect to \(N(a+b\sqrt {-2})=a^2+2db^2\ge 0\).

For \(x,y\in R,\ y\ne 0\), find such that

\[ x=qy+r,\ N(r)<N(y) \]

\[ \frac {x}{y}=c_1+c_2\sqrt {-2}\in \Q (\sqrt {-2})\quad c_1,c_2\in \Q \]

So take \(q_1,q_2\in \Z \) such that

\[ \abs {c_1-q_1}\le \frac 12,\quad \abs {c_2-q_2}\le \frac 12 \]

Set \(q=q_1+\sqrt {-2}q_2\in \Z [\sqrt {-2}]\). Then

\[ \frac {x}y=q+\alpha \quad \alpha \in \Q (\sqrt {-2}) \]

so \(x=qy+\alpha y\). Let \(r=\alpha y\in \Z [\sqrt {-2}]\). We need to show

\[ N(r)=N(\alpha y)<N(y) \]

Since \(\alpha =\alpha _1+\alpha _2\sqrt {-2}\) with \(\abs {\alpha _1},\abs {\alpha _2}\le \frac 12\),

\[ N(r)=N(\alpha y)=N(\alpha )N(y)\le N(y)\left (\frac 12^2+2\frac 12^2\right )=\frac 34 N(y)<N(y) \]

\(p=uQ^2\) then for \(R^\times \),

\[ u\in R^\times \iff \exists v\quad uv=1 \]

then \(N(u)=1=a^2+2b^2\) for \(u=a+b\sqrt {-2}\). \(b=0\) gives \(a^2=1\) so \(a=\pm 1\). Therefore \(R^\times =\{\pm 1\}\) and \(p=\pm Q^2\).

\(p\) ramified if and only if \(p\mod d\), so \(p=2\). In fact \(2=-(\sqrt {-2})^2\). \(\sqrt {-2}\) is irreducible because its norm is 2 (prime) and \(2\) is the only prime that ramifies.

\[ p=uQ\bar {Q}\iff \left (\frac {-2}p\right )=1 \]

\(3,11,17,19\) split and the remaining primes \(5,7,13,23\) are inert.

If \(p=uQ\bar {Q}\) then

\[ N(p)=p^2=N(Q)^2 \]

and

\[ N(Q)\ge 0 \]

so \(p=N(Q)\).

Let us find an element \(a+\sqrt {-2}b\) of norm \(p\). Then

\[ a^2+2b^2=p\implies \abs {a},\abs {b}\le \sqrt {p} \]

For \(p=3\) we have \(a,b=\pm 1\). \((1+\sqrt {-2})\) has norm 3, and in fact

\[ (1+\sqrt {-2})(1-\sqrt {-2})=3 \]

For \(p=11\), \(\abs {a},\abs {b}\le 3\). By trial and error, \(3+\sqrt {-2}\) is a solution and

\[ (3+\sqrt {-2})(3-\sqrt {-2})=11 \]

For \(p=17\) we have \(a=3\) and \(b=2\) to give

\[ 17=(3+2\sqrt {-2})(3-2\sqrt {-2}) \]

For \(p=19\) we have \(a=1\) and \(b=3\) to give

\[ 19=(1+3\sqrt {-2})(1-3\sqrt {-2}) \]

Let us find the factorisation of \(8+5\sqrt {-2}\). To do this, we find the norm, we factorise it, we split the resulting primes with the previous procedure and then put it back together.

\[ N(8+5\sqrt {-2})=8^2+2\cdot 5^2=114=2\cdot 3\cdot 19 \]

Clearly \(N(\sqrt {-2})=2\), but for the other factors we have a choice

\[ 3=N(1+\sqrt {-2})\text { and }N(1-\sqrt {-2}) \]

To check which divides \(8+5\sqrt {-2}\) just calculate.

\[ \frac {8+5\sqrt {-2}}{1+\sqrt {-2}}=\frac {(1-\sqrt {-2})(8+5\sqrt {-2})}3 =6-\sqrt {-2}\in \Z [\sqrt {-2}] \]
